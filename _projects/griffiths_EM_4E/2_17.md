## Part A

To pick the right Gaussian surface, let's image what the electric field would look like. We know that the electric field will shoot off perpendicular to the surface of the charge density. So it probably looks like this.

The easiest Gaussian surface to work with this for this type of electric field is a cylinder shown below for the region inside the slab, $$y<d$$.

<div class="row">
    <div class="col-sm mt-3 mt-md-0">
        {% include figure.html path="assets/img/griffiths_EM_4E/2-17-2.jpeg" title="Problem 2.16" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" %}
    </div>
</div>

Since we're dealing with a uniform charge density, $$Q_\text{enc}$$ is fairly simple,

$$
\begin{align*}
\oint_{S}\mathbf{E}\cdot d{\mathbf{a}}&={\frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}}}Q_{\mathrm{enc}} \\
\left( E \right) \left( 2\pi r l \right)&= \frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}} \left( \rho \pi r^2  \right) \\
\mathbf{E} &= 0.
\end{align*}
$$
