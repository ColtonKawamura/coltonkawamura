Part A

We’re told to use Equation 2.27 in the text,

\[\begin{align*} V({\bf r})&=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{q_{i}}{r_{i}} \label{1} \tag{1} \\ &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\left( \frac{q}{z^2+(d/2)^2}+\frac{q}{z^2+(d/2)^2} \right)\\ &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}2\left( \frac{q}{z^2+(d/2)^2} \right) \end{align*}\]

Now we verify we can get the electric field from this potential,

\[\begin{align*} \mathbf{E} &=-\nabla V\\ &= - \frac{2q}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\nabla \left( \frac{1}{z^2+(d/2)^2} \right) \\ &= - \frac{2q}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}} \left[ 0+0+\frac{\partial}{\partial z} \left( \frac{1}{z^2+(d/2)^2} \right)\hat{z}\right]\\ &= - \frac{2q}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}} \left[-\frac{1}{2}(z^2+(d/2)^2\right]^{-3/2}2z\hat{z} \\ &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\frac{2q z}{\left[z^{2}+(d/2)^{2}\right]^{3/2}}\;{\hat{ z}}. \end{align*}\]

This is the same as Example 2.1 in the text.

Part B

Since this is continuous, we’ll use Equation 2.30 in the text. We integrate along the length of the line segment from \(-L\) to \(+L\),

\[\begin{align*} V&=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\int\frac{\lambda({\bf r}^{\prime})}{r}\,d l^{\prime}\\ &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\;\int_{-L}^{L}\;\frac{\lambda\;d x}{\sqrt{z^{2}+x^{2}}}\\ &= \frac{\lambda}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\ \mathrm{ln}(x+\sqrt{z^{2}+x^{2}})\biggr]^{L}_{-L}\\ &= \frac{\lambda}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\ln\left[\frac{L+\sqrt{z^{2}+L^{2}}}{-L+\sqrt{z^{2}+L^{2}}}\right]. \end{align*}\]

Now let’s make sure we can get the electric field we found in Example 2.2 in the text,

\[\begin{align*} \mathbf{E} &=-\nabla V\\ &= -\frac{\lambda}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\nabla \left( \ln\left[\frac{L+\sqrt{z^{2}+L^{2}}}{-L+\sqrt{z^{2}+L^{2}}}\right]\right) \\ &=- \frac{\lambda}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\left[ 0+0+\frac{\partial}{\partial z} \left( \ln\left[\frac{L+\sqrt{z^{2}+L^{2}}}{-L+\sqrt{z^{2}+L^{2}}}\right]\right)\hat{z}\right]\\ &= - \frac{\lambda}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}} \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{z^2+L^2}+L}\ \frac{1}{2}(z^2+L^2)^{-1/2}2z-\frac{1}{\sqrt{z^2+L^2}-L}\frac{1}{2}(z^2+L^2)^{-1/2}2z\right)\hat{z}\\ &= \frac{\lambda z}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\frac{1}{z^2+L^2}\left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{z^2+L^2}-L}-\frac{1}{\sqrt{z^2+L^2}+L}\right]\hat{z}. \end{align*}\]

Part C

Using Equation 2.30 in the text for a charged surface,

\[\begin{align*} V&=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\int\frac{\sigma({\bf r}^{\prime})}{r}\,d a^{\prime} \\ &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\int_0^R\frac{\sigma 2\pi r}{\sqrt{z^2+r^2}}dr\\ &= \frac{\sigma 2\pi }{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\left( \sqrt{R^2+z^2} -\sqrt{0^2+z^2}\right)\\ &= \frac{\sigma 2\pi }{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\left( \sqrt{R^2+z^2} -z\right). \end{align*}\]

Now check to make sure the equals the field that we got in Problem 2.6

\[\begin{align*} \mathbf{E} &=-\nabla V\\ &= \frac{\sigma 2\pi }{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\left[ 0+0+\frac{\partial}{\partial z} \left( \left[ \sqrt{R^2+z^2} -z\right]\right)\hat{z}\right]\\ &= \frac{\sigma 2\pi }{4\pi\epsilon_{0}}\left[ \left({\frac{1}{2}}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{R^{2}+z^{2}}}}2z-1 \right)\hat{z}\right]\\ &= \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_{0}}\left[1-\frac{z}{\sqrt{R^{2}+z^{2}}}\right]\hat{z}. \end{align*}\]

Part D

If we changed to one of the charges to \(-q\) in Figure 2.34a of the text, then we all points along the \(z\)-axis would have a potential of zero.

We know this because if we look at Equation \ref{1}, the potential at ever point is the sum. If we change one of those two \(-q\), the sum will be zero. And the resulting field on that line is

\[\begin{align*} \mathbf{E} &=-\nabla V\\ &= -\nabla 0\\ &= 0. \end{align*}\]

However, this only provides information regarding the potential and electric field along that axis.
