Part A

For outside the sphere using Gauss’s law, we create a Gaussian surface that has symmetry with the electric field caused by the charge distribution. Here, we’ll use a spherical Gaussian surface.

The blue is the Gaussian surface. Pretend the white circle is a solid sphere.

With this surface, our \(Q_\text{enclosed}\) is all of the charge of the solid sphere. Using Gauss’s law,

\[\begin{align*} \oint_{S}\mathbf{E}_\text{outside}\cdot d{\mathbf{a}}&={\frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}}}Q_{\mathrm{enc}} \\ \left( E_\text{outside} \right) \left( 4\pi r^2\right)&= \frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}}\left( \rho \frac{4}{3} \pi R^3\right)\\ E_\text{outside} &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}}\left( \rho \frac{R^3}{3r^2}\right)\\ \mathbf{E}_\text{outside} &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}}\left( \rho \frac{R^3}{3r^2}\right)\hat{r}. \end{align*}\]

Part B

For inside the sphere, we now draw a spherical Gaussian surface with \(r<R\).

Here, the \(Q_{\mathrm{enc}}\) is a fraction of the total charge of the total spherical charge.

\[\begin{align*} \oint_{S}\mathbf{E}_\text{inside}\cdot d{\mathbf{a}}&={\frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}}}Q_{\mathrm{enc}} \\ \left( E_\text{inside} \right) \left( 4\pi r^2\right)&= \frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}}\left( \rho \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3\right)\\ E_\text{inside} &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}}\left( \rho \frac{r^3}{3r^2}\right)\\ \mathbf{E}_\text{inside} &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}}\left( \rho \frac{r}{3}\right)\hat{r}. \end{align*}\]
