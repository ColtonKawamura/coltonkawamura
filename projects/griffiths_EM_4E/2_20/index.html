Part A

Let’s review the criteria for electric fields.

  1. They have no curl.
  2. They are conservative.

Let’s put these criteria to use.

\[\begin{align*} \nabla \times \mathbf{E} &= \nabla \times k[x y\hat{\bf x}+2y z\,\hat{\bf y}+3x z\,\hat{\bf z}] \\ &= k \begin{vmatrix} \hat{x} & \hat{y} & \hat{z} \\ \partial/\partial x & \partial/\partial y & \partial/\partial z \\ xy & 2yx & 3zx \end{vmatrix} \\ &=\text{not zero.} \end{align*}\]

Since the curl of this alleged electric field is non-zero, we can just stop here - it’s not an electric field.

Part B

First we curl this electric field to see if the curl is zero,

\[\begin{align*} \nabla \times \mathbf{E} &= \nabla \times k[y^{2}\hat{\bf x}+(2x y+z^{2})\hat{\bf y}+2y z\hat{\bf z}]. \\ &= k \begin{vmatrix} \hat{x} & \hat{y} & \hat{z} \\ \partial/\partial x & \partial/\partial y & \partial/\partial z \\ y^2 & (2xy+z^2) & 2yz \end{vmatrix} \\ &=0. \end{align*}\]

Alright, you passed our first test, electric field. But let’s see if you’re conservative.

We were hinted in the problem to find the potential \(V\) for that electric field. So let’s do that. First, we start at the origin, and then “walk” to the point \((x',y',z')\) one axis at a time and calculate the the total electric field that was aligned with that step \(\int \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l}\).

Let’s start by “walking” in the \(x\) direction first while holding the \(y\) and \(z\) values \(0\),

\[\begin{align*} V_x &= -\int_0^{x'} \mathbf{E}_x \cdot d \mathbf{x} \\ &= -\int_0^{x'} \left. (ky^2)\hat{x}\right|_{y=z=0} \cdot dx \hat{x} \\ &=0. \label{1} \tag{1} \end{align*}\]

That’s all the potential we gained from walking on the \(x\) path. Now let’s walk from that point to the \(y'\) point,

\[\begin{align*} V_y &= -\int_0^{y'} \mathbf{E}_y \cdot d \mathbf{y} \\ &= -\int_0^{y'} \left. k(2x y+z^{2})\hat{y} \right|_{x=x',z=0} \cdot dy \hat{y} \\ &= -\int_0^{y'} k(2x' y+0^{2}) dy \\ &= -k2x'\int_0^{y'}ydy \\ &= -kx'y'^2. \label{2} \tag{2} \end{align*}\]

Now we hold those \(x\) and \(y\) values constant and walk up to the \(z\) point,

\[\begin{align*} V_z &= -\int_0^{z'} \mathbf{E}_z \cdot d \mathbf{z} \\ &= -\int_0^{z'} \left. k(2yz)\hat{z} \right|_{x=x',y=y'} \cdot dz \hat{z} \\ &= -k2y'\int_0^{z'}z dz \\ &= -ky'z'^2. \label{3} \tag{3} \end{align*}\]

Now we add up the potential we gathered during the two path by adding equations \ref{1}, \ref{2}, and \ref{3}. I’m dropping the primes because I don’t want to type them anymore,

\[\begin{align*} V_\text{total} &= V_x+V_y+V_z \\ &= 0-kxy^2-kyx^2 \\ &= -k(xy^2+yx^2). \end{align*}\]

Now time to apply the criteria. If this alleged electric field is the real thing, we should be able to take the negative gradient of it’s potential (from any path) and get the original field,

\[\begin{align*} -\nabla V_\text{total} &= k\Bigl[\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(x y^{2}+y z^{2}\right)\hat{\bf x}+\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x y^{2}+y z^{2})\,\hat{\bf y}+\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(x y^{2}+y z^{2}\right)\hat{\bf z}\Bigr]; \\ &= k[y^{2}\hat{\bf x}+(2x y+z^{2})\,\hat{y}+2y z\,\hat{\bf z}] \\ &= \mathbf{E}. \end{align*}\]
