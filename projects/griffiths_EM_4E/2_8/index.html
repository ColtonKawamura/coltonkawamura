Part A

We’re hinted at using our results from Problem 2.7 to start with this problem.

What we have in the problem, is a solid sphere, but in physics, that’s just a fancy way of saying a bunch of concentric spherical shells.

A point inside a sphere at a distance \(r\) from the center is located outside a sphere of radius \(r\), but inside a spherical shell of radius \(R-r\) as shown below.

I exaggerated the space between the inner sphere and ourself is negligible. I refuse to apologize for my artistic skills.

For evaluating the electric field here, we can leverage our answer from parts B and C of Problem 2.7.

From Part B of Problem 2.7, contributions to the electric field inside a spherical is zero.

From Part C of Problem 2.7, contributions to the electric field outside a sphere is the same as a point charge concentrated at the center,

\[\begin{align*} \mathbf{E}_\text{inner sphere}&= \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_{0}}\frac{q_\text{inner sphere}}{z^{2}}\hat{z}. \tag{2} \label{2} \end{align*}\]

But, we are free the place the \(z-\)axis wherever we want, and this will just become the \(r\) axis from symmetry, so we can express equation \ref{2} as,

\[\begin{align*} \mathbf{E}_\text{inner sphere}&= \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_{0}}\frac{q_\text{inner sphere}}{z^{2}}\hat{z}. \tag{3} \label{3} \end{align*}\]

We can relate the fraction of the charge that is in the inner sphere \(q_\text{inner sphere}\) to the charge of the sphere as a whole,

\[\begin{align*} q_\text{inner sphere}&=\frac{V_\text{inner sphere}}{V_\text{whole sphere}}q\\ &=\frac{\frac{4}{3}\pi r^{3}}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^{3}} q\\ &=\frac{ r^{3}}{ R^{3}}q \end{align*}\]

Replacing this with value for \(q_\text{inner sphere}\) in equation \ref{3}, it becomes,

\[\begin{align*} \mathbf{E}_\text{inner sphere}&= \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_{0}}\frac{\left( \frac{ r^{3}}{ R^{3}}q \right)}{r^{2}}\hat{r} \\ &= \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_{0}}\frac{ r q }{R^{3}}\hat{r}. \end{align*}\]

Part B

This part is way easier.

From our reasoning from Part C of Problem 2.7, contributions to the electric field outside a sphere is the same as a point charge concentrated at the center. Thus, for a point outside our sphere,

\[\begin{align*} \mathbf{E}&= \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_{0}}\frac{q}{r^{2}}\hat{r}. \end{align*}\]
